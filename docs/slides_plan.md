Slide 2 – Problem Statement: Gaps in Delhi’s First Line of Justice
    Goal: Clearly define the real-world challenge, rooted in Delhi and India-wide evidence.

    You can structure as 3–4 big bullets:

    1. Unequal access and bias at the first contact

    Delhi High Court has had to order action against officers for harassing a Dalit man who tried to get an FIR registered, showing how caste and status can still influence access to justice.

    National surveys (Status of Policing in India) show marginalized communities and low-income groups report the lowest trust in police and higher perception of discrimination.

    2. FIR non-registration and weak documentation

    A Lok Sabha reply on Delhi recorded over a hundred complaints in just months about non‑registration of FIRs, treating it as dereliction of duty.
    ​

    In Delhi cybercrime, only 407 FIRs were registered in 2023 despite around 450 complaints every single day, due to verification delays and process bottlenecks.
    ​

    3. Knowledge and process gaps, especially under new laws

    One year into Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita, police across states admit they are still “unlearning and relearning” sections, risking misclassification and errors under pressure.

    BPR&D’s own training modules on “attitudinal change” acknowledge serious gaps in legal knowledge, behaviour, and citizen-centric communication.
    ​

    4. Manpower overload and call-handling burden

    CAG and Parliamentary committee reports on Delhi Police highlight manpower shortages and 12–15 hour duties; station staff juggle paperwork, footfall, and incessant calls.

    This leaves less time for field investigation, community policing, and high-quality follow-up.

    Close with a line:

    “Result: who you are, where you live, and when you call can still decide whether you are heard, how seriously your complaint is taken, and how accurately it is recorded.”

Slide 3 – Problem Deep-Dive: The Five Critical Gaps We Target
    Goal: Explicitly map your 5 core issues with one strong sentence each.

    Use a 2-column infographic layout: “Ideal Police Behaviour” vs “On-ground Reality”.

    1. Unbiased hearing & response

    Ideal: Every caller, from MP to ragpicker, gets the same respectful, patient, structured hearing in their language.

    Reality: Courts and surveys show complaints from Dalits, women, migrants and informal workers are more likely to be delayed, dismissed or mishandled.

    2. FIR registration when legally required

    Ideal: If facts disclose a cognizable offence, FIR is promptly registered, regardless of “gravity” in officer’s opinion.

    Reality: Non-registration is common enough that Delhi and other courts regularly order action against officers and reiterate that preliminary inquiries cannot replace FIR in many categories, especially SC/ST cases.

    3. Strong, up-to-date legal knowledge at the front-line

    Ideal: Every officer on the phone can instantly recall correct sections, procedures, and citizen rights under Bharat Nyay Sanhita.

    Reality: Transition to new criminal codes plus low ongoing training means many officers must “double‑check sections” or rely on outdated mental models.

    4. Accurate e-statement and evidence capture

    Ideal: Victims and accused can give detailed statements without needing to visit the thana, in a language and tone that makes them comfortable.

    Reality: Despite Delhi Police piloting AI‑based Hindi voice typing for FIRs, most police stations still depend on hurried, in‑person dictation and handwritten notes.
    ​

    5. Overburdened human officers stuck on calls

    Ideal: Officers spend maximum time on field work, investigation, and community engagement.

    Reality: Manpower shortages and increasing call volumes glue officers to phone lines for routine queries, blank calls, and status checks, lowering quality of response where it matters.

    Punchline you can put in bold:

    “We are trying to fix the first mile of justice for Delhi – where most trust is either built or broken.”

Slide 4 – Solution: NyaySetu – AI Inbound & Outbound Calling Agent for Delhi Police
    Goal: High-level solution description – simple, crisp.

    What is NyaySetu?

    A multilingual AI calling agent integrated with Delhi Police helplines that listens, understands, and speaks in Indian languages, logs structured complaints, assists with legal reasoning, and routes or escalates cases in real time.

    Who does it serve?

    Citizens: any dialect, any socio‑economic background, via phone, WhatsApp, or IVR.

    Police: beat officers, PS staff, control room operators, and senior leadership for analytics.

    Core value propositions (tie to your 5 points):

    Unbiased hearing: Same respectful script and flow for everyone, independent of caste, gender, or accent.

    FIR support: Auto‑drafts FIR-ready narratives for cognizable cases, reducing silent refusals.

    Law intelligence: Embedded KB of Bharat Nyay Sanhita + Delhi Police SOPs, accessed via RAG in milliseconds.

    E‑statements: Captures full statements over voice, with adaptive questioning and emotional tone control.

    Freeing officers: Filters and resolves routine calls; escalates only what truly needs a human.

    Why now?

    Delhi Police has already piloted AI-based Hindi voice typing and online e‑FIR; NyaySetu is the logical next step: moving from “voice typing” to “voice‑first justice workflow automation.”

Slide 6 – Architecture: High-Level System & Data Flow
    Goal: Give them a systems‑thinking diagram in words that you or a notebook LLM can easily convert into a diagram.

    Think of 4 layers + 3 user groups.

    Actors:

    Citizens (phone/WhatsApp/web).

    Police personnel (control room, PS, beat officers).

    Supervisors & policymakers (CP office, MHA/MCD dashboards).

    Layers for the diagram:

    Channel Layer

    Telephony gateway / IVR provider (e.g., Exotel/Twilio/Ozonetel).

    WhatsApp Business API, SMS gateway, web widget or Delhi Police app integration.

    Speech & NLU Layer

    ASR: Sarvam Speech / Bhashini Indic-Conformer for Hindi + regional languages.

    Language detection + translation (Bhashini NMT / AI4Bharat IndicTrans) to normalize input into a canonical internal representation.

    NLU / LLM engine (can be Vertex AI, Bedrock, or on-prem Llama‑based models fine‑tuned on police dialogue).

    Reasoning & Orchestration Layer

    Conversation manager: handles context, memory, escalation thresholds, and fallback to human.

    RAG layer: queries KB (laws, SOPs, scheme details, jurisdiction info) stored in Elasticsearch / vector DB / managed KB (Vertex AI RAG, AWS KB).

    Workflow engine:

    Classifies call type (emergency, non‑emergency, information, follow‑up).

    Generates e‑statement/FIR drafts.

    Creates/updates cases in CCTNS or Delhi Police internal CMS.

    Data & Analytics Layer

    Case database: complaint metadata, caller profile (age band, gender, vulnerability category), status, and actions.

    Analytics engine: sentiment from call transcripts, call resolution times, FIR conversion rates, language mix, area-wise issue clustering, and “first response quality” scores.

    Dashboards:

    Station/beat dashboards for workload and responsiveness.

    City‑level view for Delhi Police HQ and MCD to see safety trends.

    Data flow bullets you can show in a numbered graphic:

    Citizen calls → telephony → ASR (Sarvam/Bhashini) → text.

    Text + context → LLM + RAG → response + action recommendations.

    Conversation manager → speaks back via TTS (Sarvam/Bhashini/other) and logs structured complaint/e‑statement.

    Workflow engine → pushes case to CCTNS / PS system + notifies relevant officer.

    Analytics engine → continuously updates dashboards and sends hyper‑local alerts / micro‑accountability notifications.
